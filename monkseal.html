the challenge

Sharing information in an engaging way

The Waikiki Aquarium, established in 1904 in Honolulu, Hawaii, is one of the oldest public aquariums in the U.S. I aimed to redesign the Hawaiian monk seal page to make it more visually appealing and engaging.

the solution

Incorporating visuals and attempting gsap

The objectives were to add in additional visual elements, while sharing information for both tourists and residents in a way that was appealing and digestible for audiences of all ages.

Mapping it Out

The first step was to analyze the current website and see what information could be consolidated to increase readability.

I visited other national aquarium website's, in particular The Monterey Bay Aquarium, to prioritize key features that would need to be present in my redesign.

I concluded that condensing and combining text-heavy sections onto a single scroll webpage, and supplementing the information with outstanding visuals, was the best option for this project. I also wanted to make this site feasible to code, which is why I kept the sections minimal.

Adding Some Style

The next phase was to experiment with different colors and typefaces to bring my wireframe to life. I experimented with a couple of different color palettes and typographic pairings that matched the feel of The Waikiki Aquarium.

Typography

DM Serif was chosen for the headings and subheadings. The delicate serifs and ball terminals added a touch of elegance hinting towards type in scientific textbooks, fitting for an aquarium. For web use, I substited with Libre Baskerville.

Raleway is a geometric sans-serif typeface, that balances DM Serif well with its more welcoming appearance. I felt this would be more suitable for heavier body company, to make readability lighter for all audeinces.

Color

I wanted the website to mimic the beautiful ocean and habitat of the monk seal. Using Hawai'i's beautiful ocean as a reference, I chose a gradient of light to dark blues and created an organic wave graphic to incorporate in my design.

Final Logo

The final logo I chose was the guanaco, a relative of the alpaca that is commonly found in Patagonia.

Through my research I learned that they are native to Patagonia, and are an iconic symbol of the region known for their adaptability to harsh environments, which was also a fitting symbol and message that represented Patagonia, the company.

Hidden in the body of the guanaco is the silhouette of the iconic Fitz Roy mountain, which the founder of Patagonia attempted to ascend.
